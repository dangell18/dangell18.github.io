French & German

“French and German” people descend from ancient Alpine-Celtic and Germanic populations, and inhabit an area extending from the Netherlands to Austria – roughly corresponding to the extent of Charlemagne’s Frankish Kingdom in the Middle Ages. Estimates place Charlemagne himself in the family trees of all modern Europeans, possibly many times over. Genetically and geographically, the French and Germans are at the heart of Europe.

French & German people have lived at a crossroads in human migration for millennia, and their genetics reflect that history. Celtic languages and culture first appeared in Central Europe, extending from the British Isles to the Black sea by the 3rd century BCE. But the rise of the Roman Empire spelled the downfall of a Celtic-speaking Europe, and today, Celtic languages can only be found along the far western periphery of their former territory.

With the Western Roman Empire in decline, Germanic tribes conquered vast territories from the North Sea to North Africa. Although Germanic languages did not replace the Latin-based languages spoken in what is now France and Belgium, recent studies found evidence that the migrations of Germanic peoples from the 4th to the 6th centuries had a more significant impact on Europe’s DNA than did the earlier expansion of the Roman Empire.



British & Irish

The British Isles have been continuously occupied by humans for the last 11,000 years, but more recently, the people of the Isles have left their genetic fingerprints around the world, following centuries of nautical exploration, colonization, and immigration. In the early 20th century, the Republic of Ireland won its independence from the United Kingdom, but the people of these nations share a common genetic heritage rooted largely in Celtic, Anglo-Saxon, and Viking migrations from northwestern Europe.

In a recent landmark project (called “People of the British Isles”), researchers identified a number of distinct genetic clusters within the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland, all strongly correlated with regional borders. As the researchers increased the resolution of their analysis, the first unique cluster to separate from the rest of the UK was the Orkney Islands off the northeastern coast of Scotland, followed by Wales. At a much higher resolution, the study was able to uncover a genetic boundary between neighboring Cornwall and Devon in the southwestern tip of England (which may come as no surprise to the people of these counties, who observe radically different culinary routines: in Devon, clotted cream is applied to scones first, before the jam. In Cornwall, it’s the opposite).
